The VLTI is a unique discovery machine for exploring the Universe at the Highest Angular Resolution. 
SHARPS will open new parameter space in terms of wavelength coverage, spectral resolution,
sensitivity, and high-contrast observations at small inner working angle, 
enabling exciting new science applications in the following areas:

### Exoplanets: 
High-contrast dual-field capability will allow spectroscopy of giant and rocky planets in the 0.03–1" range, including systems from GAIA DR4 and RV surveys. 
The unprecedented astrometric precision, on microarcsecond scales, will enable the search for closer-in yet undetected planets or binary exoplanets/exomoons. 
The instrument will access highly complementary spectral features to the existing VLTI facility instruments, including novel molecular tracers for exoplanet atmospheric retrieval.

### Planet Formation and Star Formation: 
In the field of accretion and mass ejection, the instrument will cover unique accretion- and outflow-tracing lines that will allow reconstructing the 3-dimensional velocity field and the magnetic field topology in the mass accretion/ejection region around young stars and around forming planets. Obtaining precision orbits and measuring the spin-orbit alignment for large samples of binary and exoplanet systems will provide crucial constraints on the dynamical processes that shape the architecture of star and planetary systems. 

### Stellar Astrophysics: 
Shorter wavelengths, combined with vibration control at the VLTI, will provide improved astrometric precision enabling the detection of intermediate-mass black holes, and resolved microlensing studies probing the lower mass gap between neutron stars and black holes. Velocity-resolved imaging of stellar convection and stellar wind will advance our understanding of stellar structure and allow us to derive precision ages for Galactic Archaeology.

### Extragalactic Astrophysics and Cosmology: 
SHARPS will resolve the detailed gas kinematics in the broad-line region of active galactic nuclei, providing Black Hole masses at intermediate redshift and removing systematic uncertainties for Black Hole masses at higher redshift. Together with a dedicated reverberation-mapping campaign, this should provide tight constraints on the Hubble constants and allow solving the ongoing debate on the Hubble tension.
